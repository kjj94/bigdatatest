Splitting and Fragmenting

The Implications of Migration on Households as a Collective Unit

Kailin Jones and Angela Loescher-Montal

We talk about migration in terms of individuals, about one person crossing a border, but we believe treating migration as an individual endeavor often loses the nuance and reliance of migration for the household as a unit. How is the household as a whole affected by migration?



We have interviews from almost 5,000 individuals representing distinct households across Honduras, El Salvador, and Guatemala. While migration travel is thought of as singular, the effects of migration ripple through the entire family.










In our data set, families are composed of many different types, ranging from bi-parental (1), single parent (2), extensive (3), or other (4). Here, you can see the type of household broken down by the size of our household is 4.46 people. We can see that there are an average of 4.46 people per household.










By dividing the breaking up of families across families that have migrated and those that have not, we can begin to unpack how migration affects across the families.
